The history of computing hardware in the late twentieth century is often told through the lens of the personal computer, but the real story is one of radical improvement in the speed and cost of memory. A decade before the Intel 4004, the state of the art in commercially available memory was still core — physical ferrite toroids threaded by hand on copper wires. Each core stored a single bit, and a thousand bits cost roughly what a family sedan cost today. By the early 1970s integrated circuit memory had crossed the threshold of cost parity and core was essentially dead for mainstream computing. What replaced it was not a single technology but a family of them: bipolar memories for the fastest caches, MOS dynamic memories for bulk store, and, eventually, charge-coupled devices for early camera sensors and line buffers.

In the Soviet Union, the same transition happened on a delay of roughly five years, which is interesting because the Soviets had access to Western technical journals and were by most accounts capable of building the fabs. The delay was largely political and economic: the central planning system did not reward the aggressive product cycles that allowed Western memory vendors to ship new parts at sharply lower prices every nine months. A fab that had been set up to produce a particular 4K DRAM would continue producing it long after the rest of the world had moved on to 16K and 64K parts, because rewiring the production line would have disrupted the five-year plan that had been budgeted against the older chip.

Sorting is one of those problems that seems trivial until you try to make it fast. The theoretical limit for comparison-based sorting is well-known — n log n comparisons, for a problem size of n — and any sorting algorithm that hits that bound is optimal in the information-theoretic sense. In practice, however, the constant factors matter enormously, and the right sort for a given situation depends on things like the distribution of the input, whether the data fits in cache, whether equal keys should remain in their original order, and whether the sort is expected to be interrupted part way through. Radix sort, which is not comparison-based, can be substantially faster than any n log n sort for numeric data with a bounded range of values, but it pays a price in memory locality on large inputs and it is harder to make stable without a second pass.

The origin of the word `bug` in the sense of a software fault is typically attributed to Grace Hopper and the famous moth taped into the logbook of the Harvard Mark II computer, but Hopper herself pointed out that the word had been used in the same sense by engineers going back to at least Edison, who wrote about the difficulty of finding bugs in his circuits in the 1870s. The moth story is true — there really was a moth, and it really was taped into the logbook with the annotation `first actual case of bug being found` — but the word was already common usage. The interesting thing about the moth story is not the etymology but the meta-level observation: by 1947 the word was so embedded that finding a literal insect inside a computer was novel enough to be worth noting for posterity.

A compiler is, at its heart, a program that reads another program and produces a third program that is semantically equivalent to the second but expressed in a different language. This definition is deceptively simple, because almost every word in it hides a lifetime of subtlety. What does it mean for two programs to be semantically equivalent when one of them is written in a language with lazy evaluation and the other is not? What does it mean to read a program when the input language has ambiguity that can only be resolved by consulting a type system that is itself defined by the program? In practice, compilers get built not because the theoretical problem has been solved but because compilers that work most of the time on most of their input are good enough, and the cases where they do not work are often more interesting than the cases where they do.

The debate about whether indentation or braces are the right way to delimit blocks in a programming language is probably the closest the software field has come to a religious war that outsiders can understand. On one side are the people who believe that code should look like code — that is, that the visual structure of a program should mirror its logical structure, and that using whitespace to convey meaning is elegant and readable. On the other side are the people who believe that making whitespace semantically significant is a trap that causes obscure bugs the moment a piece of code is passed through a tool that doesn't preserve it exactly — a text editor that silently trims trailing spaces, a version control system that converts tabs to spaces, a copy-paste operation that loses indentation. Both camps have a point. The actual answer is that languages that make good choices about whitespace tend to be the ones where the compiler is aware of the choice and fights for it, and languages that make bad choices tend to be the ones that added whitespace-significance as an afterthought.

When distributed systems researchers talk about consensus they usually mean the problem of getting a set of machines to agree on a single value in the presence of network partitions and individual machine failures. The classic result, attributable originally to Fischer, Lynch, and Paterson in 1985, is that no deterministic consensus algorithm can guarantee progress in the fully asynchronous model, meaning that any such algorithm must either be allowed to make probabilistic progress or must assume something about the network that is slightly stronger than pure asynchrony. In practice, every consensus algorithm used in production today either adds timeouts — which is a weak synchrony assumption in disguise — or uses randomisation. Paxos adds timeouts. Raft adds timeouts. Nakamoto consensus uses proof of work, which is in a sense the ultimate randomisation device. The FLP impossibility is real, but it does not constrain practical systems as much as a naive reading of it might suggest, because the adversary that FLP allows is unreasonably pessimistic relative to real networks.

Kernels are measured by their worst case, not by their average. A system that handles ten million requests per second with a median latency of eighty microseconds but a 99.99th percentile latency of four seconds is almost always a worse system than one that handles eight million requests per second with a median of one hundred microseconds but a 99.99th percentile of fifty milliseconds. The reason is that high-percentile latency propagates upward through the call graph: if one subsystem has a fat tail, and it is called by another subsystem, the second subsystem also has a fat tail, and every consumer of that second subsystem sees the fat tail. Systems that care about their worst case spend a lot of time investigating what causes the high-percentile outliers, and the answer is almost always some form of shared state that occasionally becomes contended, plus garbage collection in the cases where the language has garbage collection.
