The history of artificial intelligence began in earnest with the work of Alan Turing in the 1940s and 1950s. Turing's seminal 1950 paper 'Computing Machinery and Intelligence' posed the question 'Can machines think?' and introduced what became known as the Turing Test. The field saw rapid growth in the 1956 Dartmouth Conference where John McCarthy coined the term 'artificial intelligence'. Early researchers including Marvin Minsky Allen Newell Herbert Simon and Claude Shannon laid foundational work in symbolic reasoning game playing and information theory. The 1960s saw progress in natural language processing with programs like ELIZA. The 1970s focused on expert systems and knowledge representation. The 1980s brought the second AI winter as research funding dried up amid disappointment with the limits of expert systems. The 1990s saw the rise of statistical methods and machine learning. IBM's Deep Blue defeated chess champion Garry Kasparov in 1997 marking a milestone. The 2000s saw the renaissance of neural networks driven by larger datasets faster compute and architectural innovations like convolutional neural networks. The 2010s witnessed the deep learning revolution with breakthroughs in image recognition speech recognition and machine translation. AlexNet's 2012 ImageNet victory was a watershed moment. The development of transformer architecture in 2017 by researchers at Google paved the way for large language models. GPT-3 in 2020 demonstrated remarkable capabilities. The early 2020s saw the explosion of generative AI with ChatGPT making AI accessible to the general public. Models grew larger reaching hundreds of billions of parameters. Applications expanded across writing coding image generation and scientific research. Now in the mid-2020s the field grapples with questions of safety alignment and governance as AI systems become increasingly capable and integrated into society.
