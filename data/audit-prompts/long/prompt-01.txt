Last Tuesday the city council finally voted on the proposed transit overhaul that had been debated, modified, and delayed for nearly eighteen months. The plan, drawn up after extensive consultation with neighbourhood associations, transit advocates, and the regional planning agency, called for an expansion of dedicated bus lanes along three major corridors, a redesign of the central transfer hub to prioritize wheelchair accessibility, and an experimental fare-capping pilot intended to make daily transit costs more predictable for low-income riders. Critics argued the price tag was too high for a transit system that already underperformed its peer cities; supporters countered that the underperformance itself was the reason the investment was urgent.
